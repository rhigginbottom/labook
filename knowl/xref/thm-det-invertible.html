Theorem 4.2.11.

For any \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) over \(\ff\text{,}\) \(A\) is invertible if and only if \(\det(A) \neq 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

For \(A \in M_n(\ff)\text{,}\) let \(B\in M_n(\ff)\) be the unique RREF of \(A\text{.}\) From Proposition 3.3.6, we know there exist elementary matrices \(E_1, \ldots, E_k\) such that
\begin{equation*} A = E_1 \cdots E_k B\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can apply Theorem 4.2.10 repeatedly to see that
\begin{equation*} \det(A) = \det(E_1 \cdots E_k B) = \det(E_1)\cdots \det(E_k)\det(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(\det(E_i) \neq 0\) for each \(i\) by Proposition 4.2.8, we conclude that \(\det(A) \neq 0\) if and only if \(\det(B) \neq 0\text{.}\)
We now assume that \(A\) is invertible. Theorem 3.3.8 tells us that \(B = I_n\text{,}\) so \(\det(B) \neq 0\text{.}\) This proves one direction of the theorem.
We will prove the contrapositive of the other direction of the theorem. We now assume that \(A\) is not invertible, which (again by Theorem 3.3.8) means that \(B \neq I_n\text{.}\) Specifically, \(B\) must have fewer than \(n\) pivots, which means that \(B\) must have at least one row of zeros. By Proposition 4.2.1 we have \(\det(B)=0\text{.}\) Therefore, we must also have \(\det(A)=0\text{.}\)
